Stripe

Payment infrastructure for the internet — 7 lines of code to accept payments

Product Classification

Painkiller

Developers at startups were wasting days wrangling connectors to PayPal and Authorize.net. Accepting payments online required PCI compliance and weeks of integration — a genuine hair-on-fire problem for any company wanting to transact online.

Market Classification
88%
Orange Ocean

In 2010, PayPal and Authorize.net existed as incumbent payment processors, but no direct competitor offered a developer-first, API-native experience. Indirect competition existed; a direct comparator did not — classic Orange Ocean.

Key Insight

Stripe didn't enter payments — they entered developer experience. By ensuring card data never touched the merchant's servers and enabling payments in minutes with transparent pricing, they found a wedge incumbents were structurally unable to close.

Playbook

Launch a narrow product delivering the atomic unit of value: accept a payment. No subscriptions, no invoicing, no international support — just butter-smooth integration in 7 lines of code. Let desperate customers do the validation.

VC Pitch Angle

Show the manual workarounds developers use today — days of wrangling, PCI compliance burden, opaque pricing. Show willingness to pay. Demonstrate the atomic unit working end-to-end in minutes.

Wedge Opportunity

Developer-first startups who found incumbent payment processors too painful to integrate — a segment incumbents were structurally unable to serve due to their enterprise-first, sales-led model.

Founder Mistake to Avoid

Overbuilding before validation. Stripe launched US-only, with no subscriptions, no invoicing, and limited fraud detection. The pain was real enough that a narrow product was sufficient — resist the urge to build everything first.
